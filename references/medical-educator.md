# Medical Educator -- Full Reference

## Role

Medical education specialist with expertise in clinical teaching, curriculum design, assessment development, and learning theory. Designs educational experiences that develop clinical reasoning, professional competence, and lifelong learning habits in medical trainees and practitioners.

## Teaching Case Design

### Structure

1. **Learning objectives**: 2-3 specific, measurable objectives per case
2. **Clinical stem**: Realistic scenario with demographics, chief complaint, history, vitals, physical exam
3. **Progressive disclosure**: Information released in stages mirroring real clinical workflow
   - Stage 1: Chief complaint + brief history
   - Stage 2: Full history + physical exam
   - Stage 3: Initial investigations (labs, imaging)
   - Stage 4: Additional workup results
   - Stage 5: Response to treatment / follow-up
4. **Decision points**: "What would you do next?" at each stage
5. **Teaching pearls**: 3-5 key takeaways
6. **Debrief**: What went well, what was missed, what to do differently

### Complexity Levels

**Novice**: Classic presentation, single diagnosis, straightforward workup. Focuses on pattern recognition and basic clinical reasoning.

**Intermediate**: Atypical features, 2-3 competing diagnoses, decision-making under uncertainty. Tests ability to discriminate between diagnoses and manage ambiguity.

**Advanced**: Multi-system involvement, evolving presentation, ethical dilemmas, communication challenges, interprofessional dynamics. Tests integration, metacognition, and professional skills.

### Cognitive Integration

Embed cognitive debiasing into case design:
- Include misleading cues that test whether learners maintain broad differentials (anchoring)
- Present an obvious first diagnosis with subtle disconfirming evidence (premature closure)
- Include a diagnosis the learner may have recently encountered in another case (availability)
- Provide one abnormal finding early, with additional abnormalities later (search satisfying)

## Learning Objectives -- Bloom's Taxonomy

### Cognitive Domain (Revised Bloom's)

| Level | Verbs | Clinical Application |
|-------|-------|---------------------|
| Remember | List, define, name, recall, identify | Normal lab values, drug classes, anatomical landmarks |
| Understand | Explain, describe, compare, summarize, interpret | Pathophysiology, drug mechanisms, disease trajectories |
| Apply | Calculate, demonstrate, use, implement, solve | CrCl calculation, Wells score, GRADE framework |
| Analyze | Differentiate, distinguish, examine, organize, deconstruct | Differential diagnosis, drug interaction analysis, study design critique |
| Evaluate | Appraise, justify, critique, defend, judge | Critical appraisal of RCT, justify treatment choice, evaluate guideline recommendation |
| Create | Design, construct, develop, formulate, synthesize | Design study protocol, construct management plan, write clinical question |

### Writing Effective Objectives

Rules:
- One active verb per objective
- Specific and measurable (avoid "understand" or "know" -- not measurable)
- Aligned with assessment method
- Pitched at appropriate Bloom's level for learner stage
- Time-bound where relevant

Good: "Given a clinical vignette, the learner will calculate a HEART score and classify the patient into the correct risk category."
Poor: "The learner will understand the HEART score."

### Mapping to Assessment

| Bloom's Level | Assessment Method |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Remember | MCQ (recall), short answer |
| Understand | MCQ (interpretation), essay |
| Apply | Clinical problems, calculations, OSCE stations |
| Analyze | Case analysis, key feature questions |
| Evaluate | Critical appraisal exercise, journal club |
| Create | Research proposal, management plan, teaching session |

## Assessment Design

### Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

Format:
- Stem: clinical vignette with sufficient context (age, sex, presentation, vitals, key findings)
- Lead-in: clear question ("What is the most likely diagnosis?" not "Which of the following is true?")
- Options: one best answer + 3-4 homogeneous, plausible distractors
- All options grammatically parallel
- Avoid: "all of the above," "none of the above," negative stems, absolute terms (always/never)

Quality checklist:
- Linked to a specific learning objective
- Tests at defined Bloom's level
- Vignette is realistic and sufficient
- Only one clearly best answer
- Distractors represent common misconceptions or errors
- No cueing (grammatical, length, or logical clues to correct answer)

### Key Feature Questions

Focus on critical decision points rather than recall. Present a clinical scenario and ask: "At this point, what is the most important next step?" Tests the pivotal decisions that distinguish competent from incompetent management.

### Script Concordance Tests (SCTs)

Present ambiguous clinical scenarios. Provide new information and ask: "This new finding makes diagnosis X..." (much less likely / less likely / neither more nor less likely / more likely / much more likely). Score against expert panel responses. Tests clinical reasoning under uncertainty.

### OSCE Stations

Standardized patient encounters testing:
- History taking (targeted, efficient, patient-centered)
- Physical examination (systematic, appropriate to complaint)
- Communication (breaking bad news, informed consent, shared decision-making)
- Procedural skills (with simulation)

Design: 8-12 stations, 7-10 minutes each, standardized marking rubric, trained assessors, standardized patients.

## Curriculum Mapping

### Competency Frameworks

**CanMEDS Roles** (Royal College of Physicians and Surgeons of Canada):
1. Medical Expert: clinical knowledge and reasoning
2. Communicator: therapeutic relationships, information exchange
3. Collaborator: interprofessional teamwork
4. Scholar: evidence appraisal, teaching, lifelong learning
5. Professional: ethics, accountability, wellness
6. Leader: resource stewardship, quality improvement
7. Health Advocate: social determinants, population health

**ACGME Core Competencies** (US):
1. Patient Care
2. Medical Knowledge
3. Practice-Based Learning and Improvement
4. Interpersonal and Communication Skills
5. Professionalism
6. Systems-Based Practice

### Curriculum Design Principles

**Spiral curriculum**: Revisit core topics at increasing complexity across training years. Year 1: basic pathophysiology and pattern recognition. Year 2: atypical presentations and differential refinement. Year 3: complex multi-system cases, ethical dilemmas, uncertainty management.

**Integration**:
- Horizontal: across disciplines within a year (anatomy + physiology + clinical skills)
- Vertical: across years (basic science --> clinical application --> independent practice)

**Constructive alignment**: learning objectives, teaching activities, and assessment methods must all align. If the objective is "analyze," the teaching must involve analysis, and the assessment must test analysis.

### Gap Analysis

Map every teaching session to:
1. Competency domain(s)
2. Learning objective(s)
3. Bloom's level
4. Assessment method

Identify:
- Gaps: competencies or topics not covered
- Redundancies: excessive repetition without progression
- Misalignment: assessment method does not match objective level
- Missing progression: same Bloom's level across years without advancement

| Method | Best For | Bloom's Level |
|--------|----------|---------------|

## Feedback and Debriefing

### Constructive Feedback Model

1. Ask the learner's self-assessment first
2. Identify specific behaviors (not character traits)
3. Balance reinforcing feedback (what went well) with corrective (what to change)
4. Make it actionable (specific next steps)
5. Check understanding and agreement

### Debrief Framework (Simulation)

1. Reactions: How did that feel? What stood out?
2. Description: What happened? (Establish shared mental model)
3. Analysis: Why did that happen? What was the reasoning?
4. Summary: Key learning points and takeaways
5. Application: How will you apply this next time?

## Educational Scholarship

### Kirkpatrick's Four Levels of Evaluation

| Level | What It Measures | Method |
|-------|-----------------|--------|
| 1 -- Reaction | Learner satisfaction | Course evaluations, surveys |
| 2 -- Learning | Knowledge/skill acquisition | Pre/post tests, OSCEs |
| 3 -- Behavior | Practice change | Workplace observation, chart review |
| 4 -- Results | Patient/system outcomes | Clinical outcomes, quality metrics |

Most medical education evaluates at Level 1-2 only. Levels 3-4 require longitudinal study and are far more meaningful.
